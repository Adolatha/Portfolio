Protecting Belgium: Playing a Role in Active Cyber Protection

The event Protecting Belgium: Play a Role in Active Cyber Protection offered an interesting look into how cybersecurity is organised within government institutions and national defence. The session brought together speakers from several organisations involved in protecting Belgium’s digital infrastructure and highlighted the different ways cybersecurity professionals can contribute to that effort.

One of the speakers, Lt. Col. Timmie Bonneu from the Belgian Cyber Force / Cyber Command, explained how cybersecurity has become an essential part of modern defence. Today, cyber threats are treated as a serious national concern, and countries are investing more in digital protection alongside traditional defence domains such as land, sea, air, and space.

His presentation also provided a broader perspective on how cyber operations are structured. He explained that cyberspace can be understood through different layers — physical, logical, and social — and that effective cyber defence depends on understanding and protecting all of them. He also described the mission of cyber command and the different functions involved, such as protecting networks, collecting intelligence, defending systems, and supporting operations when necessary.

Another important part of the session was the connection to NATO. The talk showed how cyber defence is now part of wider international security discussions and how alliances are adapting to the reality that digital attacks can have national and strategic consequences. It was also useful to hear that there are different entry paths into this field, including civilian roles, temporary military service, and full-time military careers.

Sandro Manzo, Lead of the Fusion Centre at the Centre for Cybersecurity Belgium (CCB), then explained the role of CCB in Belgium’s national cyber strategy. Since its creation in 2015, the organisation has focused on developing national cybersecurity standards and guidelines, identifying and supervising organisations that fall under the NIS2 directive, and supporting cyber awareness across the country.

The final speaker, Tom Hermant from EgovSelect, explained how IT and cybersecurity professionals are recruited into federal organisations. His presentation helped connect the technical side of cybersecurity with the career possibilities available in the public sector.

What made this event particularly meaningful to me was the broader perspective it offered. Cybersecurity is often discussed in terms of companies and private organisations, but this session showed how it also plays a major role in national stability, public services, and collective security. It gave me a better understanding of how cybersecurity functions at a federal and international level, and that is something I found both valuable and genuinely interesting.
